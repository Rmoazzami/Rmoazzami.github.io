The Story Behind Some of My Favorite Projects

Name Chat

Recently, I've taken interest in iOS app development. Since Fall 2017 I have been involved with Georgia Tech's iOS Club, learning the foundations of iOS development, Cocoa Touch, and Apple's Design Principles. I had engineered many smaller iOS applications for hackathons and my own personal enjoyment, but it wasn't until Fall 2020 that I had an idea for an application that I wanted to share with a broader audience.

The idea was simple: everyone has a personal attachment to their name; it's their unique identifier as a person in this world. Only... it's not actually unique. Using Firebase, MessageUI, and StoreKit I developed an application that puts the user in a chat room with all the other people in the world that have their same name.

The implementation of this project was more challenging than expected. For example, it required considerations such as how to handle data persistence, what the database representation and requests would look like, what the overall design and user experience would be, and how revenue could be generated.

Furthermore, knowing that as the app has a smaller user base, the number of people with overlapping names will be low, I implemented a push notification manager using Apple's token system. So, as the app scales, people who were once in empty chat rooms will be notified when other people have joined. Another consideration was the method for ensuring safety and proper content moderation. The solution to this involved creating both reporting and blocking functionality that interacts with the users cache to retroactively block a bad actor's content. The app automatically sends reports to the moderators to review and if neccesary, eject the offender. Lastly, I wanted a mechanism for users to have premium features if they wanted them. I implemented this by creating in app purchases that interacted with Apple's StoreKit API and guidelines to allow users to have an attributed prefix in front of their display name.

After three months in development, I can proudly say that Name Chat is currently on the App Store!

Responding with the name of a show you want to see triggers another text in which you'll recieve more information such as the location, time, price, lineup, and parking.

The second aspect of this project consists of a website where Atlanta Laughs, local comics, and shows are promoted. This website was developed in ReactJs, Flask, Express.JS, and Node.JS. I'm still in the process of acquiring AtlantaLaughs.com but that's where you'll be able to find it when it's up and running! AlgoTrading

Two summers ago I became really interested in the stock market and the general idea of investing. I delved into some popular investing books to see what I could learn for myself. I ended up reading famous books such as Benjamin Graham's 'The Intelligent Investor', Peter Lynch's 'One Up on Wall Street', and Lawrence McMillan's 'Options as a Strategic Investment'.

I realized most people's strategies often involved trading when a set of parameters are met, for example if the price of the stock hits the upper bound of a Bollinger Band you sell or if it hits the lower bound you buy. Since these parameters could be easily identified algorithmically, it was a perfect time to put my computer science skills to the test

My first rendition of my algorithm trader was created at Georgia Tech's 2018 Hackathon. I used Python Numpy Arrays and Pandas to pull in stock prices from IEX Finance and indicators from Goldman Sachs' Marquee API to create a graphical representation of Bollinger Bands coupled with the Goldman Sachs' indicators given the stock ticker a user put in. The Bollinger Bands allowed the user to see if the stock price was currently being under or over valued on the market place while the Goldman Sachs indicators allowed the user to see the overall health of the company, thus allowing users to make easy and informed decisions.

In the next rendition of my algorithm trader I wanted to incorporate news data an important indicator. I assembled a team of three for the 2018 Vanderbilt Hackathon and during the course of this period we set up a SK-Learn neural network that was fed in sentiment scores derived from the Natural Language Toolkit used on news articles on the top 500 companies from the Reuters API and technicial indicators such as RSI, Stoch, and Aroon from the Alpha Vantage API.
